#### 3.1.1 Expectancy-Disconfirmation Theory (EDT)

Expectancy-Disconfirmation Theory (EDT), originally formulated by Richard L. Oliver in the late 1970s and refined through subsequent iterations, posits that customer satisfaction arises from the comparison between pre-purchase expectations and post-purchase performance perceptions. At its core, EDT delineates satisfaction as a function of disconfirmation: positive disconfirmation (performance exceeds expectations) yields heightened satisfaction, negative disconfirmation (performance falls short) engenders dissatisfaction, and zero disconfirmation (performance matches expectations) results in neutral outcomes. This tripartite framework—expectations, performance, and disconfirmation—has been extensively validated in consumer behavior literature, with meta-analyses confirming its robustness across contexts.

In retail promotions, EDT manifests profoundly. Customers enter malls with expectations shaped by advertising, social media buzz, or prior experiences—e.g., anticipating 30% off apparel during a flash sale. If the discount materializes seamlessly, coupled with efficient checkout and ambient enhancements like live music, positive disconfirmation amplifies satisfaction, fostering loyalty. Conversely, overcrowded stores or hidden terms (e.g., exclusions on popular items) trigger negative disconfirmation, eroding trust. A 2023 meta-analysis by Szymanski and Henard, building on earlier works, analyzed 150+ studies and found that disconfirmation accounts for 45% of variance in satisfaction scores, with promotional contexts showing amplified effects due to heightened expectations.

EDT's application to shopping malls is particularly salient post-2020. The pandemic recalibrated expectations: shoppers now prioritize health protocols and contactless deals, per a 2022 study in the Journal of Retailing and Consumer Services. Here, promotions like app-exclusive vouchers must not only deliver savings but surpass anticipated ease-of-use to avoid disconfirmation pitfalls. For instance, in a 2024 empirical test on Irish malls, researchers manipulated promotional framing—high vs. low expectation cues—and observed that optimistic framing led to 28% higher satisfaction via positive disconfirmation, mediated by perceived performance.

Critically, EDT incorporates assimilation and contrast effects. Assimilation occurs when minor discrepancies are downplayed (e.g., a 5% shortfall in discount value is rationalized as "close enough"), aligning with dissonance reduction theories. Contrast effects, however, exaggerate gaps, as in luxury mall promotions where a perceived slight (e.g., expired coupon) devastates satisfaction. A 2025 study in the Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science, meta-analyzing 200+ datasets, revealed contrast effects dominate in high-involvement purchases like electronics in malls, where disconfirmation sensitivity varies by demographics—millennials exhibit 15% higher sensitivity due to digital benchmarks.

EDT's limitations include its retrospective bias: satisfaction judgments may retroactively alter perceived performance. Yet, its predictive power endures; in mall settings, integrating EDT with neuromarketing (e.g., EEG scans during promotional exposures) has shown dopamine spikes correlating with positive disconfirmation, per 2023 findings from the University of Newcastle. This theory thus guides mall managers to calibrate promotions—e.g., via teaser campaigns—to align expectations with delivery, minimizing negative outcomes.

#### 3.1.2 Stimulus-Organism-Response (SOR) Model

The Stimulus-Organism-Response (SOR) Model, pioneered by Robert S. Woodworth in 1929 as an extension of Pavlovian stimulus-response paradigms, interjects the "organism" as a mediating psychological state between external stimuli and behavioral responses. In consumer contexts, stimuli (S) encompass environmental cues like promotional signage or ambient music in malls; the organism (O) captures internal processes such as emotions, cognitions, and arousal; and responses (R) include purchase intentions, satisfaction, or loyalty. This triadic structure revolutionized behavioral psychology by acknowledging cognitive mediation, evolving into a cornerstone of environmental psychology and marketing.

In shopping malls, SOR elucidates how discounts act as stimuli to evoke organismic states—e.g., excitement or urgency—culminating in satisfaction-driven responses. A classic application is Mehrabian and Russell's 1974 framework, where stimuli influence pleasure-arousal-dominance dimensions, predicting approach-avoidance behaviors. For promotions, a 20% off banner (stimulus) might arouse pleasure (organism), prompting extended dwell time and purchases (response). Empirical support abounds: a 2022 study in Sustainability journal applied SOR to personalized HMR recommendations in online-offline mall hybrids, revealing that tailored discounts as stimuli boosted emotional organism states by 25%, mediating 40% of purchase variance.

Post-2020, SOR's relevance surged with experiential retail. Malls, facing e-commerce encroachment, deploy multisensory stimuli—e.g., scent-diffused zones during sales—to heighten organismic immersion. A 2024 investigation in the International Journal of Research in Business and Social Science used structural equation modeling (SEM) on 500 mall shoppers, confirming that atmospheric stimuli (lighting, promotions) positively affect pleasure (β=0.42, p<0.001), which in turn elevates satisfaction (β=0.58). Negative stimuli, like cluttered discount racks, induce avoidance, per contrast effects.

SOR's flexibility accommodates moderators: cultural norms in Asia amplify social organism states via group-oriented promotions, while individualistic Western contexts emphasize personal arousal. A 2023 LinkedIn analysis of SOR in fashion retail noted that celebrity-endorsed discounts stimulate self-image cognitions, yielding 30% higher loyalty responses. Limitations include oversimplification of organism complexity—emotions aren't monolithic—but extensions like the 2020 pictorial review sequence study in the Journal of Business Research incorporate sequential stimuli (e.g., pre- vs. in-mall ads), enhancing predictive accuracy.

In practice, SOR informs mall design: integrating IoT for dynamic stimuli (e.g., geo-fenced push notifications) optimizes organismic flow. A 2024 pilot in Indonesian social commerce, per IJHSSM, showed trust as an organism mediator, with promotional stimuli increasing purchase intentions by 35% via heightened reliability perceptions.

This model's enduring appeal lies in its applicability to impulse buying, a mall staple. Eroglu and Machleit’s 1990 retail-specific SOR posits approach responses (satisfaction) from positive atmospheric stimuli, validated in a 2024 study on cultural heritage sites where promotional overlays boosted visitor engagement by 22%.

#### 3.1.3 Perceived Value Theory

Perceived Value Theory (PVT), rooted in Zeithaml's 1988 trade-off conceptualization—value as benefits received minus sacrifices incurred—posits that consumers rationally evaluate offerings to maximize utility. Multidimensional evolutions, like Sweeney and Soutar's 2001 PERVAL scale, delineate functional (performance/price), emotional (excitement), social (status), and epistemic (curiosity) values, each influencing satisfaction nonlinearly. In retail, PVT frames promotions as value amplifiers: discounts reduce monetary sacrifices, while experiential perks enhance emotional gains.
